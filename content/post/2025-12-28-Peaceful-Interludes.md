Winter is one of my favorite times of the year here in the shop. The quiet time between Christmas and New Year is a great time to get unfinished projects done. I like to use this time to finish projects I started during the year but never quite got around to completing, which helps keep my backlog to a minimum. Below are three projects that will have the most utility in my shop that I choose to complete.
  
### 3 Projects in One Week  
  
#### Sash Saw  
![Unfinished Sash Saw](/img/2025/12/sash-saw.jpg "Crosscut Sash Saw")  

<!--Note to self - Change Image - this is a place holder only - not mine-->
  
My sash saw is one of these unfinished projects. This will be the third backsaw I have made and it completes my working set. I made my dovetail saw (bought as a kit from Tom Calisto) and my tenon saw back when the Woodwright's School was in operation in the "Backsaw Class" August 23–24, 2013 led by Tom. I love making my own saws (and other tools) as there is a great satisfaction in using tools you have made yourself. Also, there is a legacy aspect, these tools can be passed on to your descendants so they can follow the tradition. 
 
I ordered a back saw kit from Isaac over at Blackburn Tools a while back and made the handle out of cherry. Sometimes when I get a project almost complete and run into a roadblock, I set it aside for later. That was the case with this saw as when I put it together the blade kind of went off in the wrong direction a little bit. Not having the time to troubleshoot what was wrong, I decided to let my future self resolve the issue.  

Now I’m ready to finish this project. I took a closer look at what was going wrong and figured out that all I needed to do was readjust the slot for the brass back as it was pitching the blade forcing it out of true. With a couple of adjustments, I was able to get the blade perfectly straight now it’s just a matter of drilling three holes with my carbide tip drill bit and fitting the saw nuts to the handle and finishing it. 

The next step would be to sharpen the saw plate and set the teeth. As I have sharpened this saw as a cross-cut saw, the tooth geometry is a little more difficult, and it takes a little more time to sharpen. Setting initial tool geometry is definitely a skill I’ve had to learn over the years, and I’m still not that confident, but I know I can at least get a good working saw from this. Matt Cianci published an excellent book on saw sharpening over at Lost Art Press called [Set & File: A Practical Guide to Saw Sharpening](https://lostartpress.com/collections/all-books-1/products/set-file-a-practical-guide-to-saw-sharpening). This book is an excellent guide on saw sharpening and will definitely make you a better caretaker of your saw and reduce your dependence on other people for sharpening your hand saws.

#### Carving mallets   
 bought a large and a small woodturning carving mallet kit from Woodcraft this year but never got around to turning the handles. I selected a few pieces of really nice Cocobolo from my choice wood collection to turn on the lathe. These kits will now be transformed into functional well balenced tools. I plan to do more carving in future projects, and these mallets will make a fine addition to my wood carving kit. This is a relatively easy project with a high satisfaction value.
  
![plane totes](/img/2025/12/plane-totes.png "Repaired Rosewood Totes")

#### Plane tote repairs  
Gordon Addison (he has a Facebook accout) has influenced me to get more proactive on my Stanley plane tote repairs, so I reached out to him to buy some repair pieces of Rosewood to finish repairing my three broken totes. After many discussions with Gordon, I have gained enough confidence to make the repair myself.  
  
It’s deeply satisfying to clear up unfinished projects and finally put these tools and parts into service. The saw is a joy to use, and the mallets are beautiful. I can now remove the temporary totes from my three planes and replace them with the newly repaired ones.

I’m really looking forward to all my new projects in 2026 and hope to have as much fun in the shop as I did this year. This is a great time in my life, being retired gives me much more quality shop time, and I’ve completed most of my tool acquisition, or at least 95% of it. I don’t have to spend all my time hunting for tools I need, instead, I can focus on the more enjoyable work of making things. I’m not giving up buying tools entirely, but going forward my focus will be more on using tools and less on acquiring them. I still enjoy going to tool meets, but they are now more social and less acquisition oriented.

I hope that you get as much shop time in the coming year as you would like and enjoy the time that you’re there. I wish all of you and your family a wonderful new year. See you in 2026.
